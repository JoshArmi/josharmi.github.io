Terraform Module UX

UX Is Everyone’s Responsibility

Nearly every job posting I’ve ever seen about UX is UI focused, yet the skills are needed everywhere. UX is often thought about as a vehicle for user acquisition and retention:

Better UX => More Users

Whilst true, this is short-sighted. We’re going to use Terraform module design to show how UX principles are crucial everywhere.

Why Terraform Modules?

In my day job at Contino, one thing we’re known for are Cloud Landing Zones, with the chief aim of expediting their digital transformation and cloud adoption journey. As a heavy terraform shop this normally results in us helping platform teams produce a module library to accelerate their development teams.

Through this process, UX is often forgotten about. This, unfortunately, results in a long tail of negative consquences.

It is imperative that you keep in mind who your users are, and also what they’re trying to achieve.

In this case, they’re your fellow engineers and they’re trying to move faster and simplify.

The Principles

1. Don’t make me think

A classic book in many UX designers library, Don’t Make Me Think’s core principle can be applied to modules. Make your modules self-evident and logical, favour explicitness. For example, .

2. Avoid Analysis Paralysis

Humans generally do better with fewer choices than more. A good heuristic for module design is the size of the variables file, the smaller the number of variables the easier your module is to use. An additional benefit is a significantly reducing testing scope, as the amount of possibile variable combinations is smaller.

This is analagous to designing interfaces in general programming languages, as espoused in A Philosophy of Software Design, reducing the size of your interface simplifies usage, which is directly one of the goals we’re trying to support.

3. Build To Current Requirements Not The Future

A common pitfall people fall into is designing for every usecase they can imagine, not every validated usecase they have. Taking the example of an AWS module for RDS databases, it requires an IAM role to enable detailed monitoring, often they allow for the injection of a custom IAM role, which exists purely as a maintenance burden. Adding a variable is close to a one way decision, it’s much harder to take back than it is to add later.
